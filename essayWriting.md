# Markdown Notes — 2 YouTube Videos (Essay Writing + Harvard Referencing)

## Video 1 — “How to write a good essay” (Professor Tim Wilson)

- **Link:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=liyFKUFCQno  
- **Transcript source used:** YTScribe page for this video  
- **Rough scope:** Short exam-style essay structure + how to use quotations well  

---

### 1) Core thesis (what the video says an essay *is*)

- An essay is not only **your ideas**, but also **ideas you’ve acquired** that demonstrate understanding of the subject.
- The model is framed mainly for **brief exam essays (~35–45 min)**, but the same principles scale up by expanding paragraphs.

---

### 2) The “Five-unit / Five-paragraph” model

The essay is presented as **5 units (5 paragraphs)**:

1. **Paragraph 1 — Introduction**  
2. **Paragraph 2 — Knowledge / reasons (one side)**  
3. **Paragraph 3 — Knowledge / reasons (the other side)**  
4. **Paragraph 4 — “Crunch” paragraph (the decisive argument)**  
5. **Paragraph 5 — Conclusion**  

#### What each paragraph does (high-detail)

**P1 Introduction (brief)**
- “Establish how you intend to answer the question.” Keep it short.

**P2 & P3 Body (balanced coverage)**
- The goal is to show you “know what you’re talking about.”
- For an argumentative prompt, one suggested balance is:
  - **P2:** reasons **for vs against** (main reasons)
  - **P3:** **subsidiary** reasons for/against  
  *(Flexibility is allowed.)*

**P4 “Crunch” paragraph (often the longest)**
- This is the payoff paragraph where you **clearly establish your position**.
- Often you may **concede** earlier points but show why they’re weaker/wrong, then land your argument.
- Key stresses:
  - Likely the **longest** paragraph
  - Where you “back up your facts with good quotation”
  - Argument should be **clever, logical, ordered**

**P5 Conclusion (brief + no new ideas)**
- The conclusion follows naturally from the crunch paragraph and should **not introduce anything new**.
- If you start a new idea in paragraph 5, you “might as well write a new essay.”

---

### 3) Planning: a surprisingly “negative” definition

- The only way to write an essay is to **plan it**.
- The plan is not only what goes in the essay, but also what you **will not say** — saving your best ideas for the crunch paragraph.

---

### 4) Using quotations: the “3-sentence rule”

Quotations are often misused. The strict method:

A quotation “by definition requires three sentences”:
1. **Set up** the quotation (context / why you’re quoting)
2. **Quote**
3. **Explain** the quotation (how it proves your point)

Additional constraints:
- In exam timing, you can’t use all memorized quotes — **choose carefully**.
- Keep quotes **brief**; avoid quotations longer than your own writing.

---

### 5) Practical template you can copy

**5-paragraph skeleton (argumentative prompt)**

- **P1:** Rephrase question → thesis (“I will argue X”) → roadmap (P2/P3 contrast → P4 decision)
- **P2:** Best reasons **for** (or strongest interpretation A) + 1 short quote + explanation
- **P3:** Best reasons **against** (or strongest interpretation B) + 1 short quote + explanation
- **P4:** Crunch: concede what’s reasonable → show flaw/limit → decisive reasoning + 1–2 carefully chosen quotes (3-sentence rule each)
- **P5:** Conclude: restate thesis + synthesize why P4 resolves the debate (no new evidence)

---

### 6) Video 1 checklist (fast)

- [ ] Did I balance both sides before landing my view?
- [ ] Is paragraph 4 the longest + most logically ordered?
- [ ] Does every quote follow: setup → quote → explanation?
- [ ] Does the conclusion add **zero** new ideas?

### 1) Key idea explicitly stated on the video listing

- **Logical progression matters:** one paragraph/idea should lead logically to the next.

---

### 2) Harvard Referencing — detailed notes (general “author–date” Harvard)

**Important:** Harvard referencing has institutional variants — follow your school’s required version if provided.

#### A) The 2 required components
1. **In-text citations** inside your essay  
2. **Reference list** at the end with full bibliographic details  

#### B) In-text citations (author–date)
- Standard form: **(Surname, Year)**
- If quoting or referencing a specific passage: add **page number(s)** (format varies by institution)

**Common placements**
- Author not mentioned in the sentence → put everything in brackets: **(Surname, Year)**
- Author mentioned in the sentence → only year/page in brackets: **Surname (Year) argues…**

#### C) Reference list basics
- Every in-text citation must map to **one** full reference-list entry.
- The reference list is typically ordered **alphabetically by author surname** (varies by institution).

#### D) Quick reference templates (high-utility)

**Book**  
- Author, Initial(s). (Year) *Title*. Publisher. *(Place sometimes required.)*

**Journal article**  
- Author, Initial(s). (Year) ‘Article title’, *Journal Title*, volume(issue), page range.

**Website**  
- Author/Organisation (Year or n.d.) *Page title*. Available at: URL. *(Accessed: Date)*  
*(Some Harvard variants require access date — check your institution.)*

---

### 3) Integrating “logic flow” + Harvard referencing (practical workflow)

A workflow that prevents “last-minute citation chaos”:

1. Outline first: write **topic sentences only** (each must follow logically from the last).
2. Under each topic sentence, add:
   - **Claim → Evidence (quote/stat/data) → Explanation (why it proves claim)**
3. As you add evidence, immediately add:
   - **In-text citation** next to the evidence
   - A “parking lot” draft entry for the reference list (clean formatting later)

---

### 4) Mini-checklist for Video 2 (flow + citation hygiene)

- [ ] Can I summarize the “story” of my argument in 3–5 topic sentences? (flow)
- [ ] Does each paragraph start with a claim that clearly links back to the thesis?
- [ ] Does every borrowed idea have an in-text citation?
- [ ] Does every in-text citation appear in the reference list (and vice versa)?

## Combined “Best of both videos” (1-page takeaway)

- Use a **planned structure** (Video 1’s 5-unit model) so your essay has predictable momentum.
- Ensure **logical progression** between ideas/paragraphs (Video 2’s principle).
- Make paragraph 4 (or your equivalent) the **decisive reasoning paragraph** supported by your best evidence/quotes.
- Handle quotes with: **setup → quote → explain** (3-sentence rule).
- Keep referencing simple: Harvard is **author–date in-text + reference list**.
