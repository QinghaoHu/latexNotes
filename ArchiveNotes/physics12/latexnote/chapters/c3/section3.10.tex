\section{Real Gravitatonal Potential Energy}

\subsection{Real gravitational potential Energy}
In an earlier part of this unit, we studied a formula for gravitational potential energy.
\begin{equation}
    E_g = mgh
\end{equation}

In fact, there are a few problems with our old formula:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item It assumes that $g$ is constant (it changes as the distance above the Earth's surface changes).
    \item The formula uses a reference location that we define. This is not an absolute location where $E_g$ is 0.
\end{enumerate}

So, let's derive the new equation of gravitational potential energy.\\

Consider a satellite of mass $m_s$ at a large distance $r$ from the centre of planet Earth (mass $M_e$).
\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{graph/3.10.1.png}
\end{figure}

We know the work done by a force is basically the area under the curve on a force vs. displacement graph.\\

So:
\begin{align}
    \text{Work done} &= \int_{r = R}^{r = \infty} F_g\,dr \nonumber \\
    &= \int_{r = R}^{r = \infty} \frac{
        G M_E m_s
    }{
        R^2
    }dr \nonumber \\
    &= G M_E m_s \int_{r = R}^{r = \infty} \frac{1}{R^2}\,dr \nonumber \\
    &\cdots \nonumber \\
    W &= \frac{
        G M_E m_s
    }{
        R
    }
\end{align}

The work done is equal to the increase in gravitational potential energy possessed by the satellite.
\begin{align}
    W &= \Delta E_g = E_{g\infty} - E_{gR} = \frac{G M_E m_s}{R} \nonumber \\
    E_{gR} &= \frac{
        - G M_E m_s
    }{
        R
    }
\end{align}

\subsection{Satellite}
The kinetic energy of a satellite orbiting the Earth can be calculated using this formula:
\begin{equation}
    E_k = \frac{
        G \space M_E \space m_s
    }{
        2R
    }
\end{equation}

Then we can calculate the total mechanical energy of the system:
\begin{align}
    E_T &= E_k + E_g\\
    E_T &= \frac{
        - G M_E m_s
    }{
        R
    } +
    \frac{
        G M_E m_s
    }{
        2R
    } \\
    = -\frac{
        G M_E m_s
    }{
        2R
    } \label{eq: eq4}
\end{align}

Equation \ref{eq: eq4} is the \textit{total mechanical energy} of the satellite.

\section{Escape from a Gravational Field}
\begin{definition}
    [Binding Energy] The \textbf{minimum $E_k$} required for an object to escape another object's gravitational field. For the minimum $E_k$ required by the first object, the object must reach an 
    \textbf{infinite distance away} from the second object before the second object's gravity \textbf{stops} it.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
    [Escape Energy] This is a specific type of binding energy. It is an object's binding energy when the object is originally \textbf{on the surface} of the second object.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
    The \textbf{escape energy} of an object from the Earth's surface is the minimum \textbf{additional} kinetic energy the object needs to escape the Earth's gravitational field.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}
    [Escape Velocity] The \textbf{minimum speed} an object needs, when launched vertically from an object's surface, to escape its gravitational field. 
\end{definition}

\subsubsection{Solve for Escape Velocity}
Let's assume position 2 is the point where the object escapes the planet's gravitational field, and position 1 is on the surface of that planet.
\begin{align}
    E_{M2} &= E_{M1}\\
    0 &= E_{T1}\\
    0 &= E_{k1} + E_{g1}\\
    E_{k1} &= -E_{g1}\\
    \frac{1}{2}mv_1^2 &= -\frac{G M_E m_s}{R_1}\\
    v_1 &= \sqrt{\frac{2G M_E}{R_1}} \label{eq: eq5}
\end{align}

The \ref{eq: eq5} is the formula for Escape Velocity

\subsubsection{Potential Communication Question}
Why do most rocket launches occur from locations close to the equator?\\

\indent Because on the Earth's surface, the rocket's speed is not actually 0. The rocket has whatever speed the Earth's surface has and the Earth is moving fastest at the equator. \\

Let's solve for the speed of the rocket at the equator:
\begin{align*}
    v &= \frac{2\pi R}{T}\\
    v &= \frac{2\pi (6.38 \times 10^3)}{24.0\,h}\\
    v &= \frac{2\pi (6.38 \times 10^3)}{86400\,s}\\
    v &= 463.96\cdots\,\text{m/s}
\end{align*}

How does this help?\\
Assume position 1 is on the surface and position 2 is in orbit:
\begin{align*}
    E_{M2} &= E_{M1} + E_{added}\\
    E_{M2} &= E_{k1} + E_{g1} + E_{added}\\
    E_{added} &= E_{M2} - E_{k1} - E_{g1}
\end{align*}
Because we already have initial $E_{K1}$, less $E_{added}$ is needed. As a result, less fuel will be used and the launch costs less.

\begin{remark}
    Assume that for an object on a surface, $E_K$ is always 0 unless the question explicitly says "at the equator".
\end{remark}
