When a person is standing (on Earth) there are two forces acting on them:\\

When a person is in orbit around a planet, gravity is the only force acting on them. They are in a 
\textbf{constant state of free fall}. \\

To solve this problem, we need to make sure that both forces act on the person. The forces must 
be in \textbf{opposite directions}, and one of them must be a \textbf{compression force}.\\

\subsection*{Solution 1}
The spaceship is \textbf{accelerating} uniformly in a straight line.\\

But, there are two problems:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item It runs out of fuel.
    \item As an object speeds up, its \textbf{mass increases}. If the mass increases, the net force must also increase to maintain the same acceleration.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Solution 2}
Build a very large hollow ring and set it spinning.\\

For an internal F.O.R., both the \textbf{normal force} and the \textbf{gravitational force} act on it.\\

From an internal F.O.R:
\begin{align*}
    \sum \vec{F} &= m \vec{a_{per}}\\
    F_{N} - F_{fict} &= 0\\
    F_{N} &= F_{fict}\\
    F_{N} &= m\left| a_{F.O.R}\right| \\
    F_{N} &= ma_{c}
\end{align*}

We can calculate the speed of the people:
\begin{align*} 
    F_{N} &= ma_{c}\\
    mg &= ma_{c}\\
    g &= a_{c}\\
    g &= \frac{v^2}{R}\\
    v &= \sqrt{gR}
\end{align*}

So, in the rotating-spaceship question, we can always assume:
\begin{equation*}
    F_{N} = F_{g}
\end{equation*}

We can also use
\begin{equation*}
    a_{c} = \frac{4 \pi^2 R}{T^2}
\end{equation*}

Substitute into the equation:
\begin{align*}
    g &= \frac{4 \pi^2 R}{T^2}\\
    T &= \sqrt{\frac{4 \pi^2 R}{g}}
\end{align*}
or
\begin{equation*}
    f = \sqrt{\frac{g}{4 \pi^2 R}}
\end{equation*}

\subsection{Perceived Acceleration in a Rotating Frame of Reference}
When a person is standing on the equator, there is centripetal acceleration to consider.\\

The \textbf{perceived} acceleration should be calculated as follows:

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1]

    % Draw the surface

    \draw[->, thick, red] (1,0.5) -- (1, -1) node[midway, right] {$F_g$};
    % Normal
    \draw[->, thick, blue] (1,0.5) -- (1, 2) node[midway, right] {$F_{fict}$};
    % Friction
    % \draw[->, thick, green] (1,0.5) -- (-1, 0.5) node[midway, above] {$F_f$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Now, let us calculate $\vec{a_{per}}$:
\begin{align}
    \sum \vec{F} &= m\vec{a_{per}}\\
    F_N - F_{fict} &= ma_{per}\\
    mg - m\left|a_{F.O.R}\right| &= ma_{per}\\
    a_{per} &= g - a_c
\end{align}

\subsection*{Note}
    You can usually treat Earth's surface as an inertial F.O.R. unless:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item You need to be \textbf{extremely accurate}
        \item The question states \textbf{at the equator}
    \end{itemize}
