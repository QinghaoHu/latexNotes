\section{Inclined plane with Friction}
To deal with inclined planes with friction, you need to determine the direction in which the system is likely to accelerate. 
\\

Think of this as a \textbf{tug of war} between these two forces. Whichever force has the greater magnitude will determine the direction in which the system is 
likely to \textbf{accelerate}.

\subsection{How to determine the direction that the system will likely to accelerate}
Look at the \textcolor{blue}{forces} acting on the system. (Active forces are forces that are \textit{trying to make the system move})
\textcolor{red}{Do not include internal forces} \\

Along the potential line of the system's acceleration:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Find the sum of the active forces in one direction.
    \item Find the sum of the active forces acting in the opposite direction.
\end{itemize}
Compare the magnitudes of these two force sums. The direction with the larger net active force is the direction in which the 
system is likely to accelerate.
